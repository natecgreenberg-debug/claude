# Kajabi vs Teachable for Course Creators in 2026: Which Is Worth It?

Kajabi and Teachable have been the two most recommended course platforms for independent creators for years. In 2026, both have evolved significantly — and the right choice depends more on your specific situation than most comparison guides will admit.

I've helped build courses on both platforms and used them to sell digital products across different niches. Here's an honest breakdown.

## The Fundamental Difference

**Kajabi** is an all-in-one business platform for knowledge creators. Website, courses, membership site, email marketing, funnels, podcasts — it's all there. The pitch: one subscription to run your entire online business.

**Teachable** is a course hosting and selling platform. It does courses, coaching, and digital downloads well. For anything outside that scope — email marketing, website, blog — you'll need separate tools.

This distinction matters more than any feature comparison. You're essentially choosing between an integrated platform and a specialized tool.

## Pricing

This is where most people's eyes widen.

**Kajabi:**
- Basic: $149/month (3 products, 1,000 active customers)
- Growth: $199/month (15 products, 10,000 customers, affiliate program)
- Pro: $399/month (100 products, 20,000 customers, white label)

**Teachable:**
- Free: 1 course, Teachable branding, limited features
- Basic: $39/month (5% transaction fee)
- Pro: $119/month (no transaction fees, graded quizzes, certificates)
- Pro+: $199/month (custom domain email, live lessons)
- Business: $299/month

The price difference is real. Kajabi's entry point is $149/month vs. Teachable's $39/month (or free). Whether that's worth it depends entirely on how much of Kajabi's feature set you actually use.

## Courses and Learning Experience

### Kajabi

Kajabi's course builder is polished. Video uploading, organization into modules and lessons, drip content by schedule or completion, quizzes, completion certificates — all standard and working well.

The student experience app is a genuine differentiator. The Kajabi app lets students access your courses on mobile with a clean, native app experience — without you needing to build or maintain a mobile app yourself.

Comments and community can be built into the course experience. You're not pointing students to a separate Facebook group or Discord — though you certainly can if you want.

### Teachable

Teachable's course builder covers the fundamentals — video upload, lessons, modules, drip scheduling, quizzes. The student experience is clean and functional, if less fancy than Kajabi's.

Where Teachable has improved: the coaching product. You can sell live 1:1 sessions directly through Teachable, which integrated scheduling and payments. For coaches who want an alternative to Calendly + Stripe, this is genuinely useful.

The mobile experience is more basic than Kajabi. There's a Teachable iOS app, but it's not as polished.

**Course quality comparison: Kajabi has the edge, especially for mobile.**

## Marketing and Sales Features

### Kajabi

Kajabi's marketing tools are legitimately good:

- **Email marketing** with sequences, broadcasts, tagging, and segmentation — replaces a standalone email tool
- **Landing pages and funnels** — covers basic funnel needs without a separate funnel builder
- **Pipelines** — pre-built sequences for common launch patterns (product launch formula, webinar funnel)
- **Affiliate program** — built in on Growth and Pro plans

If you're starting fresh and don't have an existing email marketing setup, Kajabi's all-in-one nature saves you $50-150/month in separate tool costs.

### Teachable

Teachable's marketing is more limited. You can run coupons, affiliates (Pro+ and up), order bumps, and payment plans. But it doesn't have native email marketing — you'll integrate with ConvertKit, Mailchimp, or similar.

For creators who already have their email marketing sorted and just need a place to host and sell courses, Teachable's simpler approach is actually fine.

**Marketing comparison: Kajabi wins if you don't have other tools; Teachable wins if you already have an email platform.**

## Website and Blog

Kajabi includes a website builder and blogging — functional but not a replacement for WordPress for serious SEO content. It's sufficient for a creator who wants a clean online presence tied to their course platform.

Teachable has very minimal website functionality. It's really just a school/course store, not a website platform.

## Transaction Fees

This is important:

- Kajabi: No transaction fees on any plan
- Teachable Basic: 5% transaction fee (on top of Stripe's ~2.9% + $0.30)
- Teachable Pro and above: No transaction fees

If you're selling on Teachable Basic and doing meaningful revenue, that 5% adds up fast. At $10K/month in course sales, you're paying $500/month in fees — more than a Kajabi Basic subscription.

This comparison flips the pricing narrative: Teachable isn't necessarily cheaper once you factor in transaction fees and the tools you'll need to add (email platform, website builder).

## My Recommendation

**Choose Kajabi if:**
- You want one platform to replace email marketing, website, and course hosting
- You're doing consistent revenue (>$3K/month) where the consolidation value is real
- You want the best mobile app experience for your students
- You're running live challenges, memberships, or podcasts alongside courses

**Choose Teachable if:**
- You already have ConvertKit, Mailchimp, or another email tool you like
- You primarily do coaching and want the integrated scheduling
- You're comfortable managing a multi-tool stack
- You're at the Pro tier and above (no transaction fees, good features at $119/month)

**Consider Systeme.io if:**
- You're launching your first course and every dollar matters
- You want all-in-one features (like Kajabi) at a fraction of the cost
- You're testing a course idea before going all-in on a premium platform

The "best" platform is the one you'll actually use consistently to create and market your content. Both Kajabi and Teachable have helped creators build six and seven-figure businesses — the platform isn't the limiting factor. Your content, audience, and marketing are.
