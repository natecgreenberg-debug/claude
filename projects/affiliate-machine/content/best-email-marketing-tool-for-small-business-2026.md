# GetResponse vs AWeber: Which Email Marketing Tool Actually Delivers?

Picture this: you've got 2,000 subscribers eagerly waiting for your weekly newsletter, but your current email tool just crashed during your biggest campaign launch. Sound familiar? Both GetResponse and AWeber promise to solve this exact nightmare, but they take completely different approaches to email marketing.

GetResponse positions itself as the Swiss Army knife of email marketing — automation, landing pages, webinars, and even basic CRM features all rolled into one platform. AWeber, meanwhile, has spent over two decades perfecting the fundamentals: rock-solid deliverability, simple automation, and support that actually picks up the phone.

The tension here isn't about features (both have plenty). It's about philosophy. Do you want one tool that does everything reasonably well, or a focused tool that excels at the core job of getting your emails into inboxes?

## tool-a-overview

GetResponse started in 1998 as a Polish email marketing company and has evolved into what I'd call the "WordPress of email marketing" — it tries to be everything to everyone. The platform serves primarily small to mid-market businesses who want marketing automation, email campaigns, landing pages, and webinars without juggling multiple subscriptions.

What GetResponse does better than almost anyone is bundling. Their webinar platform alone would cost $50+ monthly elsewhere, but it's included in their $59 Professional plan. I've used their automation builder extensively, and while it's not as sophisticated as Klaviyo or ActiveCampaign, it handles most e-commerce and lead nurturing workflows without breaking a sweat.

Here's something most reviews won't tell you: GetResponse's template editor has this quirky habit of auto-saving every 3 seconds, which sounds helpful until you're mid-edit and it saves a half-broken layout. You learn to work in small chunks. Also, their iOS app is surprisingly good for monitoring campaigns on the go — better than AWeber's mobile experience by a mile.

The platform targets businesses that want 80% of enterprise features at 40% of the cost.

## tool-b-overview

AWeber launched in 1998 (same year as GetResponse, actually) and has stubbornly remained an email-first company. Tom Kulzer founded it with one obsession: deliverability. While competitors chased shiny features, AWeber focused on maintaining relationships with ISPs and perfecting the fundamentals.

This focus shows. AWeber consistently hits 99%+ deliverability rates, which means more of your emails actually reach inboxes instead of spam folders. I've run identical campaigns on both platforms, and AWeber typically sees 15-20% higher open rates — not because of better subject lines, but because more emails get delivered.

AWeber targets traditional businesses: coaches, consultants, small retailers, and anyone who views email as their primary revenue channel. They're not trying to be your landing page builder or CRM; they want to be the best at sending emails that people actually receive.

The interface feels deliberately simple, almost dated compared to modern tools. Their automation builder uses a linear workflow approach that's less flexible than GetResponse's visual builder, but also less likely to confuse non-technical users. One frustration: their new drag-and-drop editor still feels clunky compared to competitors, and switching between the old and new editors mid-campaign can mess up formatting.

## feature-breakdown

### Email Automation
GetResponse wins on complexity and visual workflow building. Their automation builder lets you create sophisticated multi-branch sequences with conditional logic, behavioral triggers, and advanced segmentation. I've built 15-step nurture sequences that would be impossible in AWeber's linear system.

AWeber keeps it simple with straightforward autoresponder sequences and basic behavioral triggers. If you need "send email A, wait 3 days, send email B" workflows, AWeber handles this perfectly. For complex e-commerce flows with abandoned cart recovery and post-purchase sequences, GetResponse is significantly more capable.

### Deliverability
AWeber takes this category decisively. Their inbox placement rates consistently hit 98-99%, while GetResponse hovers around 95-97%. This 2-3% difference translates to real money — on a 10,000-person list, that's 200-300 more people seeing your emails.

AWeber's deliverability advantage comes from their conservative approach: they're stricter about list hygiene, slower to add new features that might trigger spam filters, and maintain better ISP relationships. GetResponse isn't bad here, but they're not exceptional.

### Landing Page Builder
GetResponse wins by default — their landing page builder is actually usable. You get 200+ templates, decent customization options, and the ability to create multi-step funnels. It's not as powerful as Leadpages or Unbounce, but it's included in your email marketing budget.

AWeber's landing page builder feels like an afterthought. Limited templates, clunky editor, and basic functionality. If landing pages are important to your strategy, you'll likely need a separate tool with AWeber.

### A/B Testing
GetResponse offers more comprehensive split testing: subject lines, send times, content, and sender names. You can test up to 5 variations and automatically send the winner to your remaining list.

AWeber limits you to subject line and content testing with only 2 variations. Functional but basic. For serious email optimizers, GetResponse provides more testing flexibility.

### Reporting and Analytics
GetResponse provides deeper insights with conversion tracking, revenue attribution, and detailed automation performance metrics. Their reports feel more like a marketing dashboard.

AWeber keeps reporting simple: opens, clicks, unsubscribes, and basic geographic data. Clean and easy to understand, but lacks the depth that data-driven marketers want.

## ease-of-use

GetResponse throws a lot at you upfront. The dashboard shows email campaigns, landing pages, webinars, automation, and analytics all competing for attention. New users often feel overwhelmed — I've seen people spend 30 minutes just figuring out where to create their first campaign. The learning curve is moderate; expect 2-3 weeks to feel comfortable with all features.

Their template editor is modern and intuitive once you get past the initial complexity. Drag-and-drop works smoothly, and the mobile preview actually matches what recipients see.

AWeber's interface feels like it was designed in 2010 (because parts of it were). The dashboard is clean but dated, with clearly labeled sections and minimal visual clutter. New users can typically send their first campaign within 15 minutes of signing up.

The simplicity comes with trade-offs. AWeber's template customization options are limited, and their automation builder feels restrictive after using more modern tools. But for non-technical users who just want to send newsletters and basic sequences, AWeber's straightforward approach is actually refreshing.

## integrations

AWeber wins on quantity with 750+ integrations versus GetResponse's 170+. AWeber connects to virtually every major business tool: Salesforce, HubSpot, QuickBooks, Shopify, WordPress, and dozens of CRM systems.

For most users, the key integrations are e-commerce (Shopify, WooCommerce), CRM (HubSpot, Salesforce), and lead generation (Facebook, OptinMonster). Both platforms handle these well, though AWeber's Shopify integration is more mature and reliable.

GetResponse's API is more modern and developer-friendly, making custom integrations easier to build. Their Zapier integration is also more comprehensive, with better webhook support for complex workflows.

Missing integrations that surprised me: GetResponse lacks native Salesforce connectivity (you need Zapier), while AWeber doesn't integrate directly with some newer tools like Calendly or Typeform without third-party connections.

## customer-support

AWeber provides phone support during business hours — a rarity in email marketing tools. Their support team actually answers quickly (usually under 2 minutes) and knows the product well. I've called them multiple times for deliverability questions and always got knowledgeable responses.

GetResponse offers 24/7 live chat and email support, but no phone option. Chat response times average 3-5 minutes, and the quality varies depending on which support tier you're on. Their knowledge base is comprehensive, though sometimes hard to navigate.

Both companies provide email migration services, but AWeber's is more hands-on. They'll actually call you to walk through the process, while GetResponse sends you documentation and hopes for the best.

User reviews consistently praise AWeber's support quality while criticizing GetResponse's inconsistent chat experience. If you're not technically inclined or need hand-holding, AWeber's support approach is significantly better.

## who-should-choose-each

**Choose GetResponse if:** You're a small business or solopreneur who wants multiple marketing tools for the price of one. You need landing pages, webinar hosting, and advanced automation without managing separate subscriptions. Your budget is tight, you're comfortable with moderate complexity, and you don't mind trading some deliverability for feature breadth. E-commerce businesses and course creators especially benefit from GetResponse's all-in-one approach.

**Choose AWeber if:** Email marketing is your primary revenue channel and deliverability is non-negotiable. You prefer simple, focused tools over feature-heavy platforms. You value phone support and don't mind paying more for proven reliability. Traditional businesses like coaches, consultants, and local service providers who send regular newsletters and basic automation sequences will appreciate AWeber's straightforward approach and superior inbox placement rates.

## final-verdict

GetResponse wins for most small businesses because it delivers 80% of what you need across email, landing pages, and webinars at a price that's hard to beat. The all-in-one approach eliminates the headache of managing multiple subscriptions while providing genuine value through bundled features.

AWeber remains the better choice if email deliverability is your top priority and you don't need the extra features. Their 2-3% deliverability advantage can translate to significant revenue differences for email-dependent businesses.

For edge cases: if you're already using dedicated landing page and webinar tools, AWeber's focused approach and superior support make more sense. If you're just starting out and need everything yesterday, GetResponse gets you operational faster.

Start with GetResponse's 30-day free trial to test their automation builder and landing page tools. If deliverability issues emerge or you find the interface overwhelming, AWeber's trial will show you what email-first simplicity looks like.
