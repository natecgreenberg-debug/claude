# ConvertKit vs Mailchimp for Creators: Which One Actually Grows Your Business?

If you're a creator trying to pick an email marketing platform, you've probably landed on the ConvertKit vs Mailchimp debate at least once. They're both popular, both well-reviewed, and both have free plans — which makes it genuinely hard to choose.

I've used both. Here's the honest breakdown.

## Who Each Tool Is Actually Built For

This is the part most comparison articles skip, and it's the most important part.

**Mailchimp** was built for small businesses and e-commerce. It started as a tool for sending newsletters to customers — people who bought your product, attended your event, subscribed to your store. That DNA still shows. The interface is designed around campaigns and lists, and the automation tools feel bolted on rather than central.

**ConvertKit** was built specifically for creators: bloggers, podcasters, course creators, YouTubers, newsletter writers. Everything about it — the tagging system, the visual automation builder, the subscriber-centric model — is designed around the idea that you're building an audience and selling digital products or services to that audience.

That difference matters more than you'd think.

## The Free Plan Comparison

Both have free tiers, but they work very differently.

**Mailchimp Free:**
- Up to 500 contacts
- 1,000 emails/month
- Basic email templates
- Limited automation (single-step automations only)
- Mailchimp branding on emails

**ConvertKit Free:**
- Up to 10,000 subscribers
- Unlimited emails
- Unlimited landing pages and forms
- No automation (you need paid for sequences and automations)
- ConvertKit branding on emails

For a creator just starting out, ConvertKit's free plan has a massive advantage in list size — 10,000 vs 500 is not a small difference. But if automations are important to you from day one, you'll hit the wall quickly.

## Email Design and Templates

This is where Mailchimp wins, and it's not close.

Mailchimp has hundreds of pre-built email templates, a drag-and-drop editor that's genuinely intuitive, and the flexibility to create visually rich, branded emails with images, buttons, and multi-column layouts. If you want your emails to look like a polished newsletter from a media company, Mailchimp gives you the tools to do that.

ConvertKit takes a very different approach. Its email editor is simple — almost intentionally sparse. The philosophy is that plain-text emails get better deliverability and feel more personal. You can add images and basic formatting, but there are no fancy multi-column templates. Some creators love this. Some find it limiting.

My take: if you're a blogger or course creator who values personal connection over visual polish, ConvertKit's simplicity works in your favor. If you're running a brand where aesthetics matter — a fashion newsletter, a design publication, a retail business — Mailchimp looks better.

## Automation: Where the Real Gap Shows Up

This is the biggest functional difference between the two platforms.

**ConvertKit's visual automation builder** is excellent. You can build sequences that branch based on what subscribers click, what tags they have, what products they've bought, or what links they've visited. It's drag-and-drop, it's visual, and it's genuinely easy to understand even if you're not technical. You can see your entire funnel on one screen.

The tagging and segmentation system is also powerful. Instead of managing multiple lists (the old-school approach), ConvertKit uses a single subscriber database with tags. Someone can be tagged as "bought course A," "interested in topic B," and "came from podcast C" all at once, and you can send hyper-targeted emails based on any combination of those tags.

**Mailchimp's automation** has improved a lot, but it still feels like a secondary feature. The journey builder exists, but it's clunkier. Segmentation is list-based, which means if you have overlapping audiences, you can end up with people in multiple lists and get charged for the same subscriber twice. This is a known frustration with Mailchimp that ConvertKit specifically designed around.

For creators building product funnels, upsell sequences, or course welcome sequences — ConvertKit is the clear winner here.

## Deliverability

Both platforms have solid deliverability. I haven't seen dramatic differences in my own sends, and most independent deliverability tests put them close together.

One edge ConvertKit has: because it encourages plain-text, personal-feeling emails, opens and engagement tend to be higher, which improves sender reputation over time. Mailchimp's more visual emails sometimes get flagged by spam filters, especially if you're using lots of images.

Neither platform will get your emails delivered if you have a bad list. Both will perform well if you maintain a clean, engaged list.

## Pricing at Scale

Once you're past the free tier, pricing matters a lot.

**ConvertKit Creator (paid):**
- $15/mo for up to 300 subscribers
- $29/mo for up to 1,000 subscribers
- $49/mo for up to 3,000 subscribers
- Scales up from there based on list size

**Mailchimp Essentials (paid):**
- $13/mo for up to 500 contacts
- Scales up, but can get expensive fast

At small list sizes, they're comparable. At larger list sizes (10k+), Mailchimp tends to get more expensive. And again — the risk with Mailchimp is accidentally paying for the same subscriber twice if they're on multiple lists.

## Integrations

Both connect to the major tools — Shopify, WordPress, Zapier, Stripe, WooCommerce. Mailchimp has more native integrations overall, which makes sense given its longer history and larger user base. ConvertKit integrates well with creator-focused tools like Teachable, Thinkific, and Podia.

If you're using tools outside the creator ecosystem — a specific CRM, a niche e-commerce platform — check both integration lists before committing.

## The Budget Alternative Worth Knowing About

If you're weighing cost seriously, [Systeme.io](https://systeme.io/?sa=sa024254513373376fa2a167f3d85d4c75c0537d67) deserves a look. It's an all-in-one platform that includes email marketing, funnel building, course hosting, and affiliate management — all on a free plan that covers up to 2,000 contacts.

For a creator who's just starting out and doesn't want to pay for three separate tools (email platform + funnel builder + course platform), Systeme.io bundles everything together. It's not as polished as ConvertKit in any single area, but the value-to-price ratio is hard to beat. Paid plans start at $27/month.

It's worth mentioning as a real alternative, especially if you're bootstrapping.

## Who Should Pick Each One

**Choose ConvertKit if:**
- You're a blogger, podcaster, course creator, or newsletter writer
- You want powerful automations built around tags and sequences
- You plan to sell digital products directly to your list
- You value deliverability over visual design
- Your list will be 1,000+ people who want to hear from you personally

**Choose Mailchimp if:**
- You run an e-commerce store or retail business
- You want polished, visually designed email templates
- You need more integrations with business tools
- You're sending to customers rather than an engaged audience
- Design aesthetics matter for your brand

**Consider Systeme.io if:**
- You want one platform for email + funnels + courses
- You're bootstrapping and want to minimize software costs
- You're comfortable with a less polished interface in exchange for value

## The Bottom Line

ConvertKit is the better choice for most creators. The tagging system, the automation builder, and the subscriber-centric philosophy are all designed around how creators actually work. Mailchimp is a solid tool, but it was built for a different use case — and you'll feel that friction when you try to build creator-style funnels and sequences.

That said, if you're not a creator and you run a more traditional business, Mailchimp's visual templates and broader integrations make it the stronger pick.

And if money is the primary constraint, [Systeme.io](https://systeme.io/?sa=sa024254513373376fa2a167f3d85d4c75c0537d67) gives you more platform for less money — it just requires more setup and has a steeper learning curve.
